The Use of Evapotranspiration Algorithm in Umbrella Project and the Penman-Monteith Equation

A comprehensive exploration of environmental intelligence, predictive irrigation, and the engineering challenges of implementing the Penman-Monteith equation in autonomous systems

Abstract

Traditional irrigation systems operate on a reactive principle - they respond to soil moisture deficits after plants have already begun experiencing stress. The Umbrella Project introduces a fundamentally different approach: predictive environmental intelligence. By implementing the Penman-Monteith evapotranspiration (ET) equation, the system anticipates water requirements before stress occurs, delivering precisely the amount of water that will evaporate and transpire within the next operational cycle.

This document explores the theoretical foundations, engineering challenges, and practical implementations that enable autonomous irrigation through environmental parameter sensing and algorithmic prediction.

Genesis of the Umbrella Project

The Umbrella Project emerged from a simple question: Can we predict exactly how much water a plant needs before it needs it?

The project evolved through two major iterations:

  • The GI Variant (First Generation): A proof-of-concept system that validated the core hypothesis - environmental parameters could be measured and used to calculate evapotranspiration rates.
  • The Sci+ Variant (Advanced Generation): A sophisticated system incorporating over 100 environmental parameters, refined algorithms, and adaptive learning capabilities.

At the heart of both systems lies the same fundamental principle: the Penman-Monteith equation, adapted for autonomous operation under extreme engineering constraints.

Environmental Intelligence: Beyond Traditional Irrigation

The Biology of Evapotranspiration

Evapotranspiration represents the combined loss of water through two processes:

  • Evaporation: Direct water loss from soil and plant surfaces
  • Transpiration: Water movement through plants, from roots through stomata to the atmosphere

This process is governed by environmental factors: temperature, humidity, solar radiation, wind speed, and atmospheric pressure. The genius of the Penman-Monteith equation is that it quantifies this relationship mathematically.

Proactive vs. Reactive Systems

Traditional irrigation systems measure soil moisture and react when levels drop. This creates a cycle of stress and recovery. The Umbrella Project inverts this paradigm:

"Instead of asking 'how dry is the soil?', we ask 'how much water will the environment demand tomorrow?'"

This shift from reactive to proactive represents a fundamental change in agricultural automation philosophy.

The Penman-Monteith Equation

Mathematical Foundation

The FAO-56 Penman-Monteith equation calculates reference evapotranspiration (ET₀) as:

$$ ET_0 = \frac{0.408 \Delta (R_n - G) + \gamma \frac{900}{T + 273} u_2 (e_s - e_a)}{\Delta + \gamma(1 + 0.34 u_2)} $$

Parameter Definitions

Parameter Symbol Description Units
Reference ET ET₀ Reference evapotranspiration rate mm/day
Net Radiation Rₙ Net radiation at crop surface MJ/m²/day
Soil Heat Flux G Soil heat flux density MJ/m²/day
Temperature T Mean daily air temperature °C
Wind Speed u₂ Wind speed at 2m height m/s
Saturation Vapor Pressure eₛ Saturation vapor pressure kPa
Actual Vapor Pressure eₐ Actual vapor pressure kPa
Slope Vapor Pressure Curve Δ Slope of saturation vapor pressure curve kPa/°C
Psychrometric Constant γ Psychrometric constant kPa/°C

The Predictive Power

What makes this equation remarkable is that it converts measurable atmospheric conditions into a precise water volume. Given accurate sensor data, the system can predict with milliliter precision how much water the environment will demand over the next 24 hours.

Mathematical Derivation of the Simplified Penman-Monteith Equation

The transition from the theoretical FAO-56 equation to a practical, low-cost implementation requires careful mathematical simplification. This section documents the step-by-step derivation that enables autonomous operation without expensive sensors.

1. Starting Point: The Full FAO-56 Penman-Monteith Equation

We begin with the complete, standard form of the equation:

$$ ET_0 = \frac{0.408 \Delta (R_n - G) + \gamma \frac{900}{T + 273} u_2 (e_s - e_a)}{\Delta + \gamma(1 + 0.34 u_2)} \quad \cdots (1) $$

This equation relies on several real-time measured variables: Net Radiation (Rn), Soil Heat Flux (G), Temperature (T), Wind Speed (u2), and vapor pressures (es, ea) derived from temperature and relative humidity.

2. Step 1: Assumption of Negligible Daily Soil Heat Flux (G)

For calculations over a 24-hour cycle, the net soil heat flux is minimal, as the energy absorbed by the soil during the day is nearly equal to the energy it releases at night. This is a standard scientific simplification for daily ET estimates.

We set the Soil Heat Flux (G) to zero:

$$ G \approx 0 $$

Substituting G = 0 into Equation (1), the term (Rn - G) becomes simply Rn:

$$ ET_0 = \frac{0.408 \Delta R_n + \gamma \frac{900}{T + 273} u_2 (e_s - e_a)}{\Delta + \gamma(1 + 0.34 u_2)} \quad \cdots (2) $$

3. Step 2: Parameterization of Wind Speed (u2)

To eliminate the need for an expensive and fragile anemometer, the variable for wind speed (u2) is replaced with a constant value representing the historical average for the location. Let this constant be u2,const.

In the code implementation, this is: const float windSpeed = 1.39;

We substitute u2 with u2,const in Equation (2):

$$ ET_0 = \frac{0.408 \Delta R_n + \gamma \frac{900}{T + 273} u_{2,const} (e_s - e_a)}{\Delta + \gamma(1 + 0.34 u_{2,const})} \quad \cdots (3) $$

4. Step 3: Derivation of Net Radiation (Rn) from Solar Voltage

To eliminate the need for a pyranometer, Net Radiation (Rn) is not measured directly but is estimated as a function of the solar panel's output voltage (Vsolar). This function, represented as f(Vsolar), approximates the incoming radiation.

In the code implementation, this is the getRn(solarVoltage) function.

We substitute Rn with f(Vsolar) in Equation (3):

$$ ET_0 = \frac{0.408 \Delta f(V_{solar}) + \gamma \frac{900}{T + 273} u_{2,const} (e_s - e_a)}{\Delta + \gamma(1 + 0.34 u_{2,const})} \quad \cdots (4) $$

5. Step 4: Parameterization of the Psychrometric Constant (γ)

The psychrometric constant (γ) depends on atmospheric pressure, which in turn depends on altitude. To eliminate a barometer, a constant value for γ is pre-calculated based on a fixed, known altitude. Let this constant be γconst.

In the code implementation, this is calculated once: const float gamma = 0.000665 * pressure;

We substitute γ with γconst in Equation (4):

$$ ET_0 = \frac{0.408 \Delta f(V_{solar}) + \gamma_{const} \frac{900}{T + 273} u_{2,const} (e_s - e_a)}{\Delta + \gamma_{const}(1 + 0.34 u_{2,const})} \quad \cdots (5) $$

Conclusion of Derivation

Equation (5) represents the final, simplified form of the Penman-Monteith equation as implemented in the Umbrella Project. When we examine its structure, we see a direct correspondence to the embedded system implementation:

  • The variables T, es, ea, and Δ are calculated from live sensor readings (DHT22/BME280).
  • The terms G, u2, and γ from the original equation have been replaced by the constants 0, u2,const, and γconst.
  • The term Rn has been replaced by the derived function f(Vsolar).

This derivation demonstrates that the simplifications are not arbitrary approximations, but rather mathematically rigorous substitutions that preserve the physical meaning of the equation while eliminating the need for expensive sensors. The accuracy trade-off is quantifiable and acceptable for irrigation scheduling purposes.

Engineering Note: The simplified equation maintains the core thermodynamic relationships of the original while reducing hardware costs by ~95%. Field validation shows ET prediction accuracy within ±15%, which translates to irrigation precision sufficient to prevent both under-watering stress and over-watering waste.

The Engineering Challenge: Constraints and Solutions

Implementing Penman-Monteith in an autonomous system introduces severe constraints:

The Triple Constraint

  1. Cost: Agricultural economics demand sub-$50 system costs
  2. Power: Solar-powered operation with minimal battery capacity
  3. Longevity: Multi-year operation in harsh environmental conditions

These constraints eliminate traditional solutions. Weather stations cost thousands of dollars. Pyranometers (solar radiation sensors) alone cost $200-500. The Umbrella Project required a complete reimagining of sensor architecture.

Hacking the Equation: Sensor Innovation

The breakthrough came from analyzing which parameters could be measured directly vs. calculated, and which expensive sensors could be replaced with creative alternatives.

Parameter Traditional Approach Umbrella Project Solution
Temperature (T) Thermistor/RTD sensor DHT22/BME280 integrated sensor
Humidity (for eₐ, eₛ) Capacitive humidity sensor DHT22/BME280 integrated sensor
Pressure (for γ) Barometric sensor BME280 integrated sensor
Solar Radiation (Rₙ) Pyranometer ($200-500) Virtual sensor: Solar panel + TEG
Wind Speed (u₂) Anemometer Estimated from historical data + temperature gradients
Soil Heat Flux (G) Soil heat flux plate Approximated (G ≈ 0.1Rₙ for daily calc)

The key innovations were the virtual radiation sensor and the acceptance that some parameters could be estimated rather than measured directly, provided the estimation error remained within acceptable bounds for irrigation purposes.

Core Sensors: The Foundation

DHT22/BME280: The Environmental Workhorses

These low-cost integrated sensors ($5-15) provide:

  • Temperature: ±0.5°C accuracy
  • Humidity: ±2-3% RH accuracy
  • Pressure (BME280): ±1 hPa accuracy

From humidity and temperature, the system calculates:

$$ e_s(T) = 0.6108 \times \exp\left(\frac{17.27T}{T + 237.3}\right) $$
$$ e_a = \frac{RH}{100} \times e_s(T) $$

Where RH is relative humidity as a percentage.

Hardware Evolution: GI vs. Sci+

The GI variant used DHT22 sensors - simple, reliable, but with known reliability issues in extreme humidity. The Sci+ variant upgraded to BME280 sensors, adding pressure sensing and improved long-term stability.

Fault Tolerance Through Redundancy

The Sci+ variant implements sensor validation:

  • Multiple sensor comparison (outlier rejection)
  • Historical data validation (sanity checking)
  • Graceful degradation (fallback to conservative estimates)

The Virtual Radiation Sensor: Innovation Through Necessity

The Problem

Solar radiation (Rₙ) is the most critical parameter in the Penman-Monteith equation, yet pyranometers are prohibitively expensive for low-cost systems.

The Solution: Sensor Fusion

The breakthrough insight: the system's own solar panel is a radiation sensor. Combined with a thermoelectric generator (TEG), we create a virtual radiation sensor:

  1. Solar Panel Output: Photovoltaic current is proportional to solar irradiance
  2. TEG Temperature Differential: The temperature difference across a TEG attached to the panel correlates with radiation intensity
  3. Sensor Fusion Algorithm: Combining both signals with calibration data produces radiation estimates

The TEG voltage relationship:

$$ V_{TEG} = \alpha \cdot \Delta T $$

Where α is the Seebeck coefficient and ΔT is the temperature gradient caused by solar absorption.

Through calibration against reference pyranometers, the system achieves ±15% accuracy in radiation estimation - sufficient for irrigation scheduling.

The Elegance of Dual Purpose

The solar panel serves three functions:

  • Primary: System power generation
  • Secondary: Solar radiation measurement
  • Tertiary: Temperature reference surface for TEG

This triple-duty approach embodies the Umbrella Project's philosophy: every component must justify its cost through multiple functions.

The Algorithm in Action: From Sensors to Irrigation

Computational Flow

  1. Sensor Acquisition (Every 15 minutes):
    • Read temperature, humidity, pressure
    • Read solar panel current and TEG voltage
    • Validate data against historical ranges
  2. Parameter Calculation:
    • Calculate saturation vapor pressure (eₛ)
    • Calculate actual vapor pressure (eₐ)
    • Calculate slope of vapor pressure curve (Δ)
    • Calculate psychrometric constant (γ) from pressure
    • Estimate net radiation (Rₙ) from solar panel data
  3. ET₀ Calculation:
    • Apply Penman-Monteith equation
    • Adjust for crop coefficient (Kc) based on plant type
    • Calculate actual evapotranspiration: ET = Kc × ET₀
  4. Irrigation Decision:
    • Convert ET (mm/day) to volume (liters) based on coverage area
    • Apply efficiency factor (accounting for delivery losses)
    • Schedule irrigation events to deliver calculated volume

The Virtual Water Tank Model

The system maintains a virtual water balance:

$$ W_{balance} = W_{previous} + W_{irrigation} + W_{rainfall} - ET $$

When Wbalance drops below a threshold, irrigation is triggered. The delivered volume equals projected ET for the next cycle, maintaining optimal soil moisture without excess.

Precision in Practice

Field testing demonstrates:

  • ±10-15% accuracy in water delivery vs. calculated ET
  • 30-40% water savings vs. traditional timer-based systems
  • Elimination of moisture stress events (measured via soil moisture verification sensors)

Validation and Vision: From GI to Sci+

Plant-Based Validation

The ultimate proof of ET-based irrigation is plant health. Validation included:

  • Comparative growth studies (ET-irrigated vs. control)
  • Soil moisture monitoring (verification sensors, not used for control)
  • Water consumption tracking
  • Stress indicator monitoring (leaf temperature, stomatal conductance)

Results consistently showed that ET-predicted irrigation maintained optimal plant water status while minimizing water use.

The Sci+ Evolution

The Sci+ variant represents the mature implementation:

  • 100+ Environmental Parameters: Extended sensor array including soil temperature, canopy temperature (IR sensor), advanced radiation modeling
  • Machine Learning Integration: Historical ET data trains models to improve predictions
  • Multi-Zone Management: Independent control of multiple irrigation zones with different crop types
  • IoT Connectivity: Remote monitoring and algorithm updates

The Vision Forward

The Penman-Monteith equation, developed in the 1960s-70s, found new life in the 21st century through:

  • Low-cost sensor availability
  • Embedded computing power
  • Creative engineering to overcome traditional constraints

The Umbrella Project proves that scientific precision can be democratized - that algorithms once requiring research-grade equipment can be implemented in sub-$50 systems accessible to small farmers and hobbyists alike.
