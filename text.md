The Hostel

The transition from a rural village to the capital city was difficult. I moved into the school hostel at age ten as an introvert, small for my age, and far from home. The hostel environment was unforgiving. The dormitory was a large room filled with double-decker beds, and I was assigned a top bunk about a meter off the ground. The nights were dominated by ragging, a form of hazing prevalent in Sri Lankan boarding institutions. The seniors did not accept my introversion. They tried to correct it through force. I was isolated, mocked, and physically assaulted. There were nights where I refused to eat, refused to sleep, and cried for hours, wanting to hear my mother's voice. Even that was difficult, as seniors would steal my pocket money and block me from using the phone.

At 2:00 AM on a Tuesday, after finally managing to contact my mother through a sympathetic warden, I broke down. My parents drove through the night from Hambantota to Colombo, speeding at 140 km/h, alarmed by my distress. Shortly after, the physical abuse escalated to the point where I was hospitalized. It became clear that I could not stay in the hostel. But my parents, committed to their jobs as educators in the rural south, could not move to Colombo. I faced a choice: return to the village or stay in Colombo and manage on my own. I chose to stay.

FIA Operating Systems

This research began on a separate foundation requiring creation of a self-sustaining platform preserving micro bio-organisms (fungi, small animals, invertebrates) for extended periods (10-20 years) without human intervention. I initiated this research in July 2025, designing the physical structure, disk, and components (included in multimedia links) to create a self-sustaining, biological, intelligent, autonomous system useful for research on microorganisms like fungi, plants, and moss (Bryophyta species). The goal was eliminating physical environmental constraints while creating a microclimate inside the system. I recognized the need to use the evapotranspiration algorithm, specifically the Penman-Monteith equation developed in the 20th century, which I discovered through a Swedish author's ResearchGate paper. This crop water-harvesting algorithm could be adapted for bio-organisms including algae, fungi, and small microorganisms by modifying variables and constants. I performed mathematical derivations to create a novel Penman-Monteith ET equation (provided in multimedia links).

The critical innovation was eliminating solar radiation and heat flux measurements. Measuring solar radiation requires precision-grade pyranometers, extremely rare in Sri Lanka, prohibitively expensive (median $2,000), and highly fragile, requiring regular calibration to maintain accuracy due to wear and contamination. I asked: could we derive solar radiation and heat flux from existing environmental sensor variables? I initially used temperature sensors (BME280) for temperature and humidity in the evapotranspiration algorithm. The BME280 sensor also provides barometric pressure, which is directly adjacent to solar radiation and heat flux as an environmental variable. I hypothesized that these three variables, temperature, humidity, and barometric pressure, could approximate solar radiation and heat flux, creating massive advantages not only for evapotranspiration derivation but for any project requiring expensive, hard-to-maintain industry-grade pyranometers. While I expected lower accuracy than pyranometers, I believed significant approximation was achievable. I created the research organization FIA (Future Is Autonomous) to brand this innovation and establish a consortium governing sensors. At research conclusion, I achieved 77-78% accuracy when comparing results with Sri Lanka Meteorological Department data derived from industry-grade pyranometers. This accuracy level is extremely valuable for industry applications including autonomous vehicle multimedia systems managing air conditioning temperatures and home HVAC systems managing temperature and humidity based on surrounding environment.

I decided to publicize this as a computer science model enabling everyone to build upon it as a library downloadable and integrable into any hardware regardless of physical constraints. Recognizing C's status as the world's fastest programming language with significantly higher physical implementation speed than Python or Java, I leveraged this speed advantage to develop the Fiaphy library, a memorable name for the technology described in my research paper. The research centers on using sensor arrays for approximating values through differential calculus. I am grateful to my friend Janith Rajavasala, Sri Lanka's International Mathematical Olympiad representative in 2025, who taught me the required differential calculus through organized Zoom sessions and in-person instruction during free periods at school, he sits next to my desk. Initially I learned derivative physics through various online resources, particularly MIT OpenCourseWare. After making five initially incorrect assumptions, I corrected variables and fluctuated parameters, observing many discrepancies and misconceptions. I corrected all assumptions and maximized accuracy while maintaining micro-priority in developing soft sensing architecture for future applications. After building the entire system (described in video multimedia links showing sensor and architecture functionality), I realized these sensors can accurately soft-sense and approximate values, having proven 77% accuracy guarantee. This is hardware-independent, and critically, I never used large language models or machine learning for training to derive output. I avoided neural networks (despite this being smart practice for approximation currently used in NASA LARC, NASA Power, and similar projects with large machine learning models trained to approximate environmental variables globally including solar radiation and heat flux) because I wanted to approach this like the human brain forms new memories without physical sensation based on past memories. I realized that sensors we label merely as temperature or humidity measurements can accomplish far more. I have proven this, which is why I formed FIA Operating Systems as an organization I intend to establish as a non-profit organization, federation, or consortium similar to the Linux Foundation, which regulates industries, maintains frameworks, and drives society forward.

I aim to make FIA Operating Systems an organization driving future sensor development. In my presentation, I highlighted a critical projection: if we research current trends, more than 90% of sensors developed before 2015 (regardless of type, environmental, medical, engineering) are projected to be discarded as waste by 2030, not due to failure but due to low efficiency, accuracy, excessive physical space consumption in consumer devices, and relegation to legacy hobby tools among small researchers and hobbyists. I depicted graphs showing how industrial adoption and hobbyist adoption (maker community) fluctuate over time and follow common sensor curves. This analysis demonstrates that sensors labeled as incapable become extraordinarily capable when combined together, their combined diversity enables them to approximate variables we cannot otherwise imagine. They can accomplish things we cannot predict when diversity increases. Understanding this, I certified and promised myself to found FIA Operating Systems Federation in the future, registering it as a Public Benefit Company (PBC), Federation, or non-profit incorporation. My technology's use cases include deriving solar radiation and heat flux anywhere, in oceans, mountains, anywhere with atmospheric air as the conducting medium (regardless of air type). If air exists, we can accurately calculate solar radiation and heat flux in a physics-based manner. Data can be transmitted to the cloud (detailed in video documentation), where APIs and databases enable anyone to download data freely. Unlike proprietary datasets charging significant fees, I observed one solar radiation and heat flux provider charging $0.52 per API call, which accumulates substantially with multiple daily requests. I want to open-source this and make it entirely free. This requires community-driven establishment of Fiaphy devices worldwide connected to internet via mobile phone Wi-Fi, sending real-time data to the cloud with black-box backup options and systematic backup protocols functioning perfectly.

My initial neuroscience research led to my core concept: using biological neural tissue as the main microprocessor, a biological logic gateway array. My first ISEF research project will involve sending basic logic signals (AND, OR, XOR, NAND) from one side of neuronal tissue via a Utah array, a sharp, micrometer-scale array plate with approximately 10,000 nodes (I plan to begin with 1,000 nodes). I will emit small electrical pulses from these signal nodes and connect them to brain slices under ethical regulations with institutional support, expecting to receive meaningful electrical pulses from the opposite side. The critical challenge is that human brain tissue requires oxygen, pH-balanced blood flow, and specific temperature (37°C), otherwise brain cells die immediately. We must simulate blood and create a blood-brain barrier between environment and neural tissue to enable communication. When sending signals from one side of neuronal tissue, we must meaningfully receive those signals from the other side. By implementing XOR or basic logic gates in the middle structure through layering, we must maintain the exact neuronal circuit without changing physical structure while using an approximately 80% dry, 20% water-containing brain slice. Since brains contain ions and electrons for electricity communication, the FPGA, with its flexible, dynamically programmable circuitry, must interface appropriately. One major challenge is programming the FPGA, for which I am learning Verilog, the programming language for FPGA development. I am using Intel Quartus Prime as my IDE and will provide Andover updates on my progress through video documentation because I want the school to understand this work.

Successfully achieving this would represent a historical milestone, enabling us to artificially use neuronal circuits from paralyzed individuals or those with significant neuron deficits to create logic circuits and valuable computational logic.
